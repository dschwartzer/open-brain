User Guide

Your Open Brain

One brain. Every AI. Here's how to use it.

You've built a personal knowledge system that lives in a database you own. Every AI you use — Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, whatever comes next — can read from and write to this brain through a single connection. Here's how to make it part of your daily workflow.

Two Ways to Capture Thoughts

1. Type in Slack

Open your #capture channel in Slack and type anything — a decision, a note about a person, an insight, a meeting takeaway. The system captures it in 5–10 seconds and replies in-thread confirming what it stored.

Example — Slack capture
You
Decided to push the product launch to April 15. QA found three critical bugs in the payment flow and we can't ship without fixing them. Marcus is leading the fix.
Open Brain
✓ Captured as decision — product
Topics: product launch, QA, payment flow
People: Marcus
Action items: Fix three critical bugs in payment flow

2. Capture from any AI

When you're already in a conversation with Claude, ChatGPT, or any connected AI, you can save thoughts without switching to Slack. Just ask the AI to remember something:

Example — capture from Claude
You
Save this to my brain: realized that our biggest bottleneck isn't engineering capacity — it's the handoff between design and engineering. We lose 2-3 days per feature in translation.
Claude
Captured as insight — product
Topics: bottleneck, design-engineering handoff, process
Action items: none
ID: 7a3f...

Searching Your Brain

This is where the magic is. Ask any connected AI to search your brain using natural language. It finds thoughts by meaning, not just keywords.

Example — semantic search from Claude
You
Search my brain for anything about people considering career transitions
Claude
Found 1 thought:

1. Sarah mentioned she's thinking about leaving her job to start a consulting business
[Type: insight | Category: career | People: Sarah | Relevance: 84%]

Key insight: You never used the words "career" or "transition" in the original thought — but semantic search connects them by meaning. This is a fundamentally different universe from Ctrl+F.

Capture Templates

These sentence starters are optimized for clean metadata extraction. Use them early to build the habit — after a week you'll develop your own patterns and won't need them.

Decision
"Decided to [action] because [reasoning]. [Who] is responsible. Next step: [what]."
Person note
"[Name] mentioned [what]. They're thinking about [context]. Follow up: [action]."
Insight
"Realized that [insight]. This matters because [why]. Implication: [what changes]."
Meeting debrief
"Met with [who] about [topic]. Key takeaway: [what]. Action items: [list]."
Task / to-do
"Need to [what] by [when]. Depends on [who/what]. Priority: [level]."

Connecting Your AI Tools

Claude Desktop

1

Settings → Connectors → Add custom connector

2

Name: Open Brain

3

URL: your MCP Connection URL (the one ending in ?key=your-access-key)

4

Enable it per conversation via the + button → Connectors

Claude Code

Run in terminal
claude mcp add --transport http open-brain \
  https://your-project-ref.supabase.co/functions/v1/open-brain-mcp \
  --header "x-brain-key: YOUR_ACCESS_KEY"

ChatGPT (paid plans)

1

Settings → Apps & Connectors → Advanced settings → Developer Mode ON

2

Settings → Apps & Connectors → Create

3

Name: Open Brain, URL: your MCP Connection URL, Auth: No Authentication

Note: Enabling Developer Mode in ChatGPT disables its built-in Memory feature. Your Open Brain replaces it — and works across every AI, not just ChatGPT.

Cursor / VS Code / Windsurf

If the client supports remote MCP URLs, paste your Connection URL directly. If it only supports local stdio servers, use the mcp-remote bridge (requires Node.js).

The Weekly Review

Every Friday, ask any connected AI:

You
Browse everything I captured this week. Group by topic, flag any unresolved action items, and surface connections I might have missed.

This becomes more valuable every week as your brain grows. Patterns emerge. Connections surface. Forgotten commitments get flagged.

Tips for Building the Habit

Lower the bar. A one-sentence thought is better than nothing. "Marcus wants to move to platform team" is a perfectly good capture. You don't need to write essays.

Capture decisions, not just information. "Decided to use Postgres instead of MongoDB because we need JOIN support" is far more valuable six months from now than "looked at databases today."

Name people. "Had a good chat with Sarah about consulting" is searchable by person. "Had a good chat about consulting" isn't.

Don't organize — just capture. The system handles classification automatically. Your job is to dump your thinking into it. Let the embeddings and metadata do the organizing.

Use it from wherever you are. In Slack? Type there. In Claude? Ask Claude to save it. In Cursor? Same. Don't switch apps to capture — that kills the habit.

The Compounding Effect

Every thought you capture makes the next search smarter and the next connection more likely to surface.

At 5 thoughts, the system is a toy. At 50, it starts finding connections you missed. At 500, your AI knows your projects, your people, your decisions, your constraints — across every tool you use. It stops asking "can you give me some background?" and starts saying "this is related to what you decided last month about the API."

The gap between "I use AI sometimes" and "AI is embedded in how I think and work" comes down to this infrastructure. Start capturing. The system does the rest.
